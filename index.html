New Yorkers Have Naloxone at Their Fingertips. They Just Don't Know It.

By Laura Duclos

“When we have somebody overdose, most of the time it’s from fentanyl.”

Billy Garcia, a case manager at Washington Heights Corner Project sees opioid overdoses increasing at an unsettling pace. He works opioid users on a daily basis, in efforts to help them maintain – and eventually get rid of – their addiction.

Garcia said one of the leading factors in opioid’s rising popularity is cost.

“Fentanyl has changed the game big time up here,” he said. “Before, there were $10 bags, now there’s $5 bags because people put fentanyl in them.”

But his mentality towards halting the epidemic is contrary to what most would think logical. The solution of Garcia and many community centers is to assist people in their addiction and not cut it out of users’ lives completely.

“There’s people who need help,” Garcia said. “Just because somebody does drugs doesn’t mean they’re not human. They need to have a place where people actually care for them."

As backward-minded as helping people do drugs may sound, New York State seems to agree. According to the New York Health Department website, the government has created public service announcements, created plans and spent thousands towards community center- based programs and for naloxone, the antidote administered when a person has overdosed on opioids and prescription pain killers.

In 2015 the State government made it legal for all residents of New York to carry and administer overdose rescue kits that contain naloxone. There are over 2,000 pharmacies in the state that carry naloxone.

740 of those are in the city alone.

Despite the efforts, fewer people than originally hoped have been trained in and carry rescue kits and community centers fear it may be for stigma.

“I feel like everyone in some way or another does drugs – it could be legal like alcohol,” Garcia said. “Anybody could lose control of their use. Anyone could overindulge and there needs to people to help them. They need to teach them to not overindulge and drink in moderation and I feel like it’s the same thing for drugs.”
